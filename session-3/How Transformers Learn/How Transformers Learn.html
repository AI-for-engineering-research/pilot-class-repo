Session 03 · AI Coding Agents for Engineering Research

Nobody writes the weights.

This demo is a complete transformer: embeddings, one attention head, unembedding, 1,152 parameters in all. It predicts the next word in a small made-up language, and it is simple enough to train entirely in this browser: real forward pass, real backpropagation, real gradient descent.

01 · The shape of the machine

A word goes in as a vector. Matrices do the rest.

The point: the whole model is a short chain of matrix multiplications. A word becomes a vector of 16 numbers; that vector is multiplied through a handful of matrices (with a softmax or two along the way); the final vector is read out as a probability for every word in the vocabulary. Stay at this altitude for now; the names come on the next screen.

What to notice
  1. Reading a word = looking up its row of numbers. Those 16 numbers are the model's entire notion of “cat”.
  2. Everything after the lookup is multiply-by-a-matrix, a handful of times. There is no grammar module hiding anywhere.
  3. The final vector holds a score for each of the 16 vocabulary words; softmax squashes the scores into probabilities.
  4. Click a different word: the input vector changes and the matrices stay put. One fixed machine processes every word.
What softmax does: the raw scores come out at any size, positive or negative. Softmax exponentiates each one and divides by the total, giving 16 positive numbers that sum to 1.

02 · The matrices, named

Seven matrices hold the model's 1,152 numbers.

The point: here are the abstract matrices from the last screen with their real names and jobs. Two read the input, four form the attention head you met in session 2, one scores the vocabulary. Training exists to fill in their entries.
What to notice
  1. WE is the dictionary, with one row per word. Wpos adds “where am I in the sentence”, so the same word at a different position gets a different vector.
  2. WQ, WK, WV, WO are session 2's attention head: what am I looking for · what do I offer · what do I carry · write it back.
  3. WU holds one output direction per word; dot the running vector against all 16 and softmax. That is the prediction.
  4. Count the entries: 256+128+128+128+128+128+256 = 1,152. The heatmaps show the live values, still random static.

03 · The task

One task: predict the next token.

The point: the text itself supplies the supervision, with no labels and no teacher. At every position the model guesses the next word, and cross-entropy L = −log p(correct) scores the guess. Every parameter, embeddings included, exists only to push that number down.

What to notice
  1. There is no answer key. Cover the next word, guess it, score the guess, repeated over the corpus. Every LLM you use trains on this same signal.
  2. The score is L = −log p(correct). Confidently right → near 0. Confidently wrong → enormous. Read e^L as the number of choices the model is hedging between; a clueless model hedges over all 16 (ln 16 ≈ 2.77 nats).
  3. Some positions are predictable and some are not: after “says” the animal determines the next word; after “the” several words remain possible. That difference becomes the dashed floor on the training screen.
The corpus is deliberately tiny and synthetic, so every quantity on the next screens is exactly computable, including the floor the loss cannot go below.

05 · Training, live

Loss falls toward the entropy floor.

The point: one rule does everything: θ ← θ − η·∂L/∂θ, applied to all 1,152 parameters at once, step after step. Watch it push the loss to the entropy floor, pull the random embedding cloud into meaningful clusters, and teach “says” to look back at the animal. None of this is programmed anywhere.

While it trains, watch for
  1. The loss dives, then flattens at the dashed line. The leftover loss equals the corpus's built-in uncertainty from the last screen; no model can go below it. Training auto-pauses when it converges.
  2. The embedding map organises itself. 16 words start as random scatter; the four animals drift into one region, the four sounds into another. Nothing in the code asks for these clusters.
  3. The attention probe sharpens. The boxed “says” row concentrates onto cat; the model discovers that “says” must look back at the animal.
  4. The sparkline snaps. p(meow) goes from ~0.06 to ~1.00 in a narrow window of steps; learning often happens suddenly.
  5. ↺ reset and train again. The same structure re-emerges from a different random start, every run.
  6. Then break it: drag η far right so the steps overshoot the valley and the loss spikes. Compare SGD vs Adam at the default η.

Extra 1 (key 8) · One update, under the microscope

Backpropagation assigns the blame.

The point: “training” repeats one procedure: run forward, measure the loss, let the chain rule (backpropagation) assign each weight its share of the blame, then nudge every weight against its gradient. Here is that story for one single number you choose.

The two cleanest links in the chain

Multiply this story by 1,152 weights per step (124 million for GPT-2, ~10¹¹ for a frontier model) and by millions of steps. Every weight in every LLM is written this way.

What to notice
  1. Click any cell in the heatmaps to pick one number out of 1,152, with its role in the network spelled out: a component of “cat”, a column of the query lens, an output direction…
  2. Its gradient is the loss's sensitivity to that one number. Backprop computes all of them in one backwards sweep of the chain rule; the bar chart shows their magnitudes.
  3. At the output, the gradient is simply prediction − truth, which is why softmax + cross-entropy is the standard pairing (open the math box).
  4. “Apply this update” moves the weight against its gradient by η·g, and every other weight moves with it. That is one full step; the training screen ran hundreds of them.
If training is still running (space bar), the heatmaps shimmer; those are the live parameter values changing under gradient descent.
Extra 2 (key 9) · The test

Did it learn the rule or memorise the corpus?

The point: four sentences were deliberately deleted from the corpus before training. If the model completes them correctly anyway, it must have learned the rule: find the animal, whatever surrounds it, and emit its sound.

What to notice
  1. Before training (or right after ↺ reset): any prompt completes to noise.
  2. After training: all four held-out prompts complete correctly. These exact sentences were never in the training data, so recall is impossible.
  3. The bars show where the probability went: essentially all of it on the right sound.
  4. Where does generalisation live? In the embedding map on the training screen: animals share a region of embedding space, so a never-seen adjective–animal combination rides exactly the same machinery as a seen one. Generalisation is geometry.
This is a minimal train/test split, the held-out set behind every model evaluation you will read or run.
Extra 3 (key 0) · Scale

From 1,152 parameters to 10¹¹.

Everything you just watched scales without changing its nature. GPT-2 small is this exact loop with d=768, 12 heads × 12 layers, LayerNorm and MLPs in the stack, AdamW instead of Adam, trained on ~8 billion tokens instead of 48 sentences. Frontier models add orders of magnitude and keep the same ideas: the loss is still −log p(next token), the update is still θ ← θ − η·∂L/∂θ.

The embeddings and attention lenses we studied in session 2 are just rows and columns of θ that this loop wrote. What scale adds is what those simple parts compose into: induction heads and in-context learning, which is where we go next.

The fine print
  • The architecture is minimal: one head, residual connection, no LayerNorm, no MLP, no multi-head. Each omitted piece matters at scale; none changes what “training” means.
  • The corpus is tiny and synthetic, so “generalisation” here means held-out combinations rather than held-out concepts, a far weaker test than real corpora pose.
  • Plain Adam/SGD on whole-sentence batches. Real training: AdamW, gradient clipping, token windows in the millions, warmup/decay schedules.
  • The PCA map is a 2-D shadow of 16-D: trust within-frame distances and ignore frame-to-frame rotation.
  • The reset seeds are curated for a visually crisp attention pattern. On some seeds the model solves the task with a diffuse pattern instead; both are valid minima of the same loss, and a warning that attention maps alone don't tell you where information flows.
